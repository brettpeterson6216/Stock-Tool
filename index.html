📚 Education ›
Core concepts every investor should know.
📊 Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio
The P/E ratio compares a company's stock price to its earnings per share. A high P/E can mean the market expects strong future growth; a low P/E may signal undervaluation or slow growth. Compare P/E to the sector average and the company's own history for context.
P/E = Stock Price ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)
💰 Earnings Per Share (EPS)
EPS measures how much profit a company generates for each share outstanding. Rising EPS over time is generally a positive sign. Watch for "diluted" EPS, which accounts for options and convertible securities — it gives a more conservative picture.
EPS = Net Income ÷ Shares Outstanding
📈 Market Capitalization
Market cap is the total dollar value of a company's outstanding shares. It helps classify companies: Mega-cap (>$200B), Large-cap ($10–200B), Mid-cap ($2–10B), Small-cap (<$2B). Larger companies tend to be more stable; smaller ones can offer higher growth potential with more risk.
Market Cap = Share Price × Shares Outstanding
🏦 Revenue vs. Profit
Revenue is the total money a company brings in from its business. Profit (net income) is what remains after all expenses, taxes, and interest. A company can grow revenue while still losing money — always look at the full picture. Gross margin and operating margin help you understand efficiency.
Net Income = Revenue − Cost of Goods − Expenses − Taxes − Interest
📉 52-Week High / Low
The highest and lowest prices a stock has traded at over the past year. A stock trading near its 52-week high may indicate strength or overvaluation; near its low may signal weakness or opportunity. Always combine with fundamental analysis — price alone tells an incomplete story.
🔄 Free Cash Flow (FCF)
FCF is the cash a company generates after spending on capital expenditures (buildings, equipment). It represents money available for dividends, buybacks, debt paydown, or reinvestment. Many analysts consider FCF a better measure of financial health than reported earnings.
FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures
⚖️ Debt-to-Equity (D/E) Ratio
D/E measures how much a company relies on debt versus shareholder equity to finance its operations. A ratio above 2.0 is generally considered high-risk, though acceptable levels vary by industry. Capital-intensive industries like utilities or manufacturing typically carry more debt than software companies.
D/E = Total Liabilities ÷ Shareholders' Equity
💵 Dividend Yield
Dividend yield shows the annual dividend payment as a percentage of the stock price. A higher yield provides income but may signal a slowing company or an unsustainable payout. Always check the payout ratio — dividends exceeding earnings are a red flag.
Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend ÷ Stock Price × 100
🎓 Adv. Education PRO ›
Advanced valuation and analysis methods used by professional investors.
🧮 Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Valuation
DCF estimates the intrinsic value of a company by projecting future cash flows and discounting them back to today using a required rate of return (WACC). If the intrinsic value exceeds the current price, the stock may be undervalued. Small changes in assumptions (growth rate, discount rate) dramatically affect the result — always run multiple scenarios.
Intrinsic Value = Σ [FCFt ÷ (1 + WACC)t] + Terminal Value
📐 EV/EBITDA Multiple
Enterprise Value to EBITDA is a capital-structure-neutral valuation multiple. It's preferred over P/E when comparing companies with different debt levels. A lower EV/EBITDA relative to peers may indicate undervaluation. Typical ranges vary widely: 8–12x for mature industrials, 20–40x+ for high-growth tech companies.
EV = Market Cap + Debt − Cash  |  EV/EBITDA = EV ÷ EBITDA
📊 Return on Equity (ROE) & ROIC
ROE measures how efficiently management uses shareholder equity to generate profit. ROIC (Return on Invested Capital) is broader — it measures returns on all invested capital (equity + debt). Warren Buffett favors companies with consistently high ROE (above 15%) with little to no debt. ROIC above WACC means value creation for shareholders.
ROE = Net Income ÷ Shareholders Equity  |  ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital
📉 Technical Analysis: Moving Averages
The 50-day and 200-day simple moving averages (SMA) are the most watched technical indicators. When the 50-day crosses above the 200-day (Golden Cross), it signals bullish momentum. When it crosses below (Death Cross), it signals bearish momentum. Moving averages smooth out short-term volatility to reveal the underlying trend.
⚡ RSI — Relative Strength Index
RSI measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes on a 0–100 scale. Above 70 is typically overbought (potential sell signal); below 30 is oversold (potential buy signal). RSI works best in range-bound markets and can give false signals in strong trending markets. Always confirm with price action and fundamentals.
RSI = 100 − [100 ÷ (1 + Average Gain ÷ Average Loss)]
📡 MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence
MACD is a trend-following momentum indicator that shows the relationship between two exponential moving averages (typically 12-day and 26-day EMA). The MACD line crossing above the signal line is bullish; crossing below is bearish. The histogram visualizes the gap between the two lines. Divergence between MACD and price can signal trend reversals.
MACD = 12-day EMA − 26-day EMA  |  Signal = 9-day EMA of MACD
🏗 Comparable Company Analysis (Comps)
Comps involves valuing a company by comparing it to publicly traded peers using multiples like P/E, EV/EBITDA, P/S, and P/FCF. Select peers with similar business models, growth rates, and margins. Apply the peer median multiple to your company's metrics to derive an implied share price range. Used alongside DCF for a range of value estimates.
🔮 Margin of Safety
Coined by Benjamin Graham, margin of safety means only buying a stock when its market price is significantly below your estimated intrinsic value — typically 20–50% below. This buffer protects against errors in your analysis, unforeseen risks, and market volatility. The wider the margin of safety, the lower the risk. "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get." — Warren Buffett
